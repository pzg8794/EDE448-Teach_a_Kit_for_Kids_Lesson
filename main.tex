{\large\bfseries\color{PrimaryRed} Requirement Connection and Teaching Context}\par
The assignment requires a 10-minute lesson using Organization for Autism Research educator resources, followed by a lesson summary, reflection, and explanation of future classroom use. I used OAR's Kit for Kids materials because they translate autism acceptance into peer-friendly language. The core story, \textit{What's Up with Nick?}, and the Friendship Tip Sheets emphasize that autistic students may communicate, learn, move, or experience the environment differently while remaining equal members of the classroom community \citep{oar2026kit,oar2026nick,oar2026tips}.

I designed the lesson for a Grade 2 technology setting because my placement showed that peer support becomes especially visible during coding and robotics. Students needed help with login steps, links, pacing, materials, noise, and partner roles. One transcript also included a moment when a learner questioned whether a visitor counted as a classmate, followed by a reminder not to make the person feel excluded \citep{placement2026robotics}. These real patterns made belonging a concrete classroom issue rather than an abstract idea.

{\large\bfseries\color{PrimaryRed} Summary of the 10-Minute Lesson}\par
The lesson was titled \textit{Many Ways to Be a Good Classmate}. The goal was: \textit{I can name two ways to include and support a classmate who communicates, learns, moves, or experiences the room differently.}

I used a visible sequence: \textbf{First}, learn one idea. \textbf{Next}, practice a classroom situation. \textbf{Last}, choose one supportive action. I explained that autism is one way a person's brain can work and that autistic people are not all the same. Communication may include speech, gestures, writing, pictures, AAC, movement, or silence. Some students may need quiet, space, clear directions, processing time, predictable routines, or a break from noise.

Using the OAR friendship guidance, I taught five actions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Invite} without forcing.
\item \textbf{Wait} for communication or an attempt.
\item \textbf{Ask} instead of guessing.
\item \textbf{Respect} space, sensory needs, tools, breaks, and privacy.
\item \textbf{Include} classmates who are new, quiet, tired, or participating differently.
\end{enumerate}

The main scenario asked what a supportive classmate could do when one student already knew how to enter a coding program, several classmates were still stuck, and another student was new to the group. Students practiced showing one step without taking the Chromebook, using a calm voice, waiting, and treating the new learner as part of the class. I closed with: \textit{One way I can be a supportive classmate is... because...}

{\large\bfseries\color{PrimaryRed} Reflection}\par
The strongest part of the lesson was its connection to ordinary classroom behavior. Students often understand that they should be kind, but they need examples of what kindness looks like during noise, frustration, unequal pacing, partner work, and technology confusion. The lesson gave those moments usable language: help without taking over, wait without rushing, ask without assuming, and include without forcing.

The placement evidence also reminded me that competence can be hidden by login friction, unclear click paths, reading demands, sensory load, or role ambiguity. A pause, repeated question, refusal, or movement away from a task should not automatically be read as inability or disrespect. It may communicate that the environment or task needs to change.

The part I would revise is pacing. Ten minutes was enough for one main idea and one scenario, but not enough for a deeper discussion of communication, sensory differences, and privacy. I would revisit one action before each partner-heavy activity so the OAR language becomes part of classroom routines rather than a one-time lesson.

{\large\bfseries\color{PrimaryRed} Puzzle Plan and EQUITAS Alignment}\par
This lesson connects to my Puzzle Plan and EQUITAS goals. The Puzzle Plan is my interdisciplinary framework for combining lived experience, educational practice, classroom evidence, data, and research rather than relying on one isolated explanation. EQUITAS is the equity-focused lens within that framework; it asks whose knowledge is included, what context is missing, and where power or bias may shape a decision.

Through this lens, no diagnosis, behavior, observation, or peer comment should stand alone. Each is one piece of a larger picture. Instead of asking only why a learner is not responding, the stronger question is what communication option, wait time, sensory support, relationship move, or environmental change might increase access and agency. The goal is not pity or compliance. The goal is dignity, participation, choice, and belonging.

{\large\bfseries\color{PrimaryRed} Future Classroom Incorporation}\par
I would use Kit for Kids during beginning-of-year community building and revisit it before coding, robotics, group projects, recess transitions, and partner troubleshooting. I would pair the OAR materials with first/next/last directions, visual choices, quiet options, partner roles, help cards, wait time, and multiple ways to respond. These supports should be available to everyone rather than added only after a learner struggles.

I would also include autistic-authored perspectives when developmentally appropriate so students do not learn about autism only through non-autistic interpretation. The message I want students to carry is simple: different communication, movement, sensory needs, or pacing do not make someone less of a classmate.

{\large\bfseries\color{PrimaryRed} Conclusion}\par
OAR's Kit for Kids materials provided a developmentally appropriate way to teach peer support inside ordinary classroom moments. My main takeaway is that positive behavioral support includes peer culture. A classroom should already know how to invite, wait, ask, respect, and include before harm happens.
